Casa Ala Blanca

白翼之家  ·  HK Associates  ·  Oro Valley, Arizona, USA
Unbuilt – Private House, Popular Choice Award
Design Reading
Casa Ala Blanca by HK Associates proposes a 2,370 square foot desert residence in Oro Valley, Arizona, conceived as a continuous white wing sheltering both interior and exterior living from the unrelenting Sonoran Desert sun. The house takes its name—"white wing house"—from a single generous canopy that extends far beyond the building's envelope, creating a shaded outdoor living zone as essential to daily desert life as any enclosed room. The open-plan kitchen, dining, and living area is oriented toward the north to capture views of the Tortolita Mountains without the burden of direct solar gain, while southern glazing is deeply recessed under the overhanging roof to permit low winter sun but exclude the near-vertical summer rays entirely. Passive environmental strategies are woven into every decision: rainwater is harvested from the large roof plane and channelled to underground cisterns for landscape irrigation; cross-ventilation pathways are embedded in the plan; thermal mass concrete floors stabilize interior temperatures across the extreme diurnal temperature swing common to desert climates. The compact footprint preserves the site's existing native Sonoran plantings—saguaro cacti, palo verde, and brittlebush—with minimal excavation. The project received the Popular Choice Award at the 2026 Architizer A+ Awards in the Unbuilt – Private House category.

Tile House

磁磚之家  ·  YN Studio  ·  Hackney, London, UK
Dezeen Featured Project – July 2026
Design Reading
Tile House by YN Studio transforms a Victorian terraced house in Hackney, east London, through a material intervention so concentrated it redefines the identity of the entire street-facing facade. The practice replaced conventional slate roof tiles with bespoke panels manufactured from recycled post-consumer plastic, compressing the logic of circular material economy into an immediately legible architectural statement. The recycled plastic tiles replicate the irregular texture and tonal variation of natural slate while carrying a provenance trace embedded in each panel: documentation of the material source is stored within the building itself. Despite the radical material substitution, the facade reads as sympathetic to the protected Victorian streetscape—a deliberate ambiguity the architects describe as "sustainable camouflage." At the rear, a single-storey extension is organised around a central full-height stairwell that pulls daylight deep into the ground-floor plan, dissolving the characteristic darkness of Victorian terrace back additions. Interior surfaces are lined with Douglas fir, chosen for its warm grain and carbon sequestration during growth. The kitchen occupies the extension's south-facing corner, glazed from worktop height to ceiling eaves. Dezeen published Tile House in July 2026 as an exemplar of sustainable retrofit within London's Victorian housing stock. Photography by French + Tye.

Vivacious

生氣蓬勃  ·  Deco Farmer Studio  ·  Hong Kong
Small Apartment of the Year – Gold (≤80 m²)
Design Reading
Vivacious by Deco Farmer Studio is a compact apartment renovation in Hong Kong that received the Gold award in the Small Apartment of the Year category (under 80 square metres) at the 2026 FRAME Awards. The commission arrived when a couple who had configured their apartment over several years learned they were expecting a child, prompting a fundamental rethinking of how the existing rooms could be redistributed to accommodate a growing family without expanding the unit's footprint. Deco Farmer Studio repositioned the bedroom, study, and storage volumes to liberate a continuous open living and dining area that reads significantly larger than its true dimensions through careful proportioning and a restrained material palette. The ceiling plane is the apartment's most expressive element: gently arching curves in smooth plaster create a domestic vault that simultaneously raises the perceived ceiling height and softens the acoustic environment for an infant. Integrated joinery runs the entire perimeter of the main living space, eliminating freestanding furniture and recovering every cubic centimetre of storage. Natural light enters from two opposing facades, establishing a cross-ventilation path that reduces reliance on air conditioning. The project's name—Vivacious—captures the intention to design not for constraint but for the liveliness of family life in its earliest, most demanding chapter.

EA House

EA 之家  ·  Bokey Grant Architect  ·  Southern Highlands, NSW, Australia
Dezeen Featured Project – July 2026  /  AIA NSW Architecture Award
Design Reading
EA House by Bokey Grant Architect is a 170-square-metre family home built on the edge of a bushland escarpment in the Southern Highlands of New South Wales, on Gundungurra Country. The project's name carries its clients—Esther and Albert—within it, a gesture of intimacy that runs through every design decision. The site sits within an extreme Flame Zone bushfire hazard classification, demanding that the architecture perform simultaneously as protective enclosure and liveable sanctuary. Bokey Grant's response is deliberately monolithic: three sides of the house present a closed, protective boundary to the surrounding bush—rammed earth walls, formed concrete, and a hardwood timber rain screen, all selected for fire resistance and thermal mass. The fourth face opens entirely through full-height glazing toward a protected garden courtyard. Inside, the section creates a continuous visual connection between ground-floor living areas and the children's bedrooms above, mediated by a full-height void that functions as both the home's social nucleus and a passive ventilation stack. The marked seasonal temperature range of the Southern Highlands is managed almost entirely through thermal mass and the shaded deep plan, without mechanical heating or cooling. Dezeen published EA House in July 2026, describing it as a "low-tech sanctuary" that reconciles fire-risk landscape with an architecture of quiet warmth.

Stone Houses Roundup

石材住宅精選  ·  Various Architects  ·  Various Locations
Dezeen Residential Roundup – July 11, 2026
Design Reading
Dezeen's July 2026 roundup of stone houses gathers six tactile residences from across the globe that demonstrate the material's enduring capacity to function simultaneously as structure, insulation, finish, and landscape extension. The selection moves from rough-hewn limestone cladding applied to a French rural renovation—where local quarry stone continues a centuries-old building tradition with a contemporary section—to precast stone block construction in a Scandinavian coastal house designed to withstand salt weathering while reading as a reinterpretation of the vernacular boathouse. A Spanish hilltop villa employs ashlar sandstone blocks in load-bearing walls that store daytime heat and release it through the night, achieving passive climate control without technology. A Japanese example uses river pebbles set in mortar as an interior floor and wall surface that recalls traditional onsen bathhouse architecture in a private domestic context. Two further houses—one in rural Portugal and one in rural Australia—use stone derived entirely from site excavation: the excavated material becomes the building fabric, eliminating the embodied carbon of transport. Across all six examples, the roundup traces stone's versatility as both structural system and decorative language, demonstrating that the material's apparent constraints—weight, permanence, limited workability—are precisely the qualities that give stone houses their durability, tactility, and sense of rootedness in place.

Cedar Park House

雪松公園之家  ·  Rerucha Studio  ·  Seattle, Washington, USA
Dezeen Featured Project – 2026
Design Reading
Cedar Park House by Rerucha Studio occupies a corner lot in the Cedar Park neighbourhood of Seattle, Washington, where the architects developed a residential typology that deliberately dissolves the conventional single-volume house into a constellation of cedar-clad pavilions arranged around a central courtyard. The project's name honours both the neighbourhood and the dominant material—Western red cedar—whose natural oil content resists Seattle's persistent moisture without surface treatment, ensuring the building's appearance will evolve gracefully through weathering rather than requiring maintenance to preserve an original state. Each detached element of the programme—sleeping, bathing, cooking, working, and sheltered outdoor living—is given its own discreet pavilion, separated by gaps that frame garden views and channel daylight into what would otherwise be the dark centre of a deep plan. The courtyard at the heart of the composition operates as a shared outdoor room connecting all pavilions without requiring passage through any single one, giving each household member an independent route through the domestic landscape. The spatial strategy produces a house whose interior total square footage does not read in any single view; instead, the resident discovers the home incrementally, moving between volumes. This episodic quality distinguishes Cedar Park House from the open-plan residential norm and restores a sense of domestic ceremony to everyday transitions between activities. Dezeen featured the project in 2026 as an alternative to the Pacific Northwest box house tradition.

Casa Diez Pies

十柱之家  ·  Studio Font  ·  Mazunte, Oaxaca, Mexico
Dezeen Featured Project – July 2026
Design Reading
Casa Diez Pies by Studio Font stands on a dramatically steep cliffside above Mazunte, Oaxaca, overlooking the Pacific Ocean along one of Mexico's most ecologically sensitive coastlines. The house takes its name—"house on ten feet"—from its ten concrete columns, which lift the 250-square-metre dwelling above the slope's surface with minimal excavation, preserving the existing topography and the native coastal vegetation that clings to the cliff face and provides critical habitat for endemic Oaxacan species. The structural choice of ten columns rather than a continuous plinth is both ecological and spatial: beneath the raised platform, the cliff continues uninterrupted, and the columns become a mediating threshold between the built volume and the live landscape below. The floor plate cantilevers beyond the column grid on the ocean-facing edge, extending the interior living space outward until the boundary between room and cliff becomes experientially ambiguous. Interior spaces are organised in a single long bar parallel to the contour lines, so that every room—bedroom, living, kitchen, bathroom—shares the same unobstructed Pacific horizon. The building section is tuned to Pacific coastal ventilation patterns: the prevailing onshore breeze enters through operable louvers on the inland wall, traverses the plan, and exits through the oceanward face, cooling the interior without mechanical assistance. Dezeen published Casa Diez Pies in July 2026 as an exemplar of ecologically embedded residential architecture on Mexico's Pacific coastal corridor.

Room 206

206 號房  ·  Studio Ryu Mitarai  ·  Yokohama, Kanagawa, Japan
FRAME Awards Featured Project – Apartment Renovation
Design Reading
Room 206 is a condominium apartment renovation in Yokohama, Kanagawa, Japan, featured by FRAME Awards for its demonstration of how a single partition wall's removal can radically transform the spatial experience and the quality of light within a constrained urban dwelling. The project takes its name from the unit number, a deliberate rejection of programmatic labelling that invites the apartment to be understood as a spatial proposition rather than a commodity floor plan. The apartment is distinguished by a characteristic protruding bay window on its east elevation—a feature common to the postwar Japanese residential building type—which the designer chose to preserve and celebrate as the renovation's primary light source. The removed partition wall had previously divided the living room from a sleeping alcove, creating two rooms each too small to function generously; with the wall gone, a single continuous space connects the bay window's eastern light to the west-facing balcony, creating a cross-illuminated room that changes character dramatically between morning and afternoon. The material palette deliberately contrasts the existing building fabric: plaster walls are returned to white; a new oak floor replaces worn carpet and visually unifies the extended space; built-in oak millwork along the north wall consolidates kitchen, study, and storage within a continuous element that disciplines the domestic programme without compartmentalising the open plan. FRAME Awards featured Room 206 as evidence that transformative residential design requires neither extensive budget nor extensive area—only precise understanding of light, proportion, and the one wall that must not stay.
